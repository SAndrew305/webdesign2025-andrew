## The Kernel: Linux (1991)

The story of Linux begins in 1991 with a Finnish student named Linus Torvalds. At the time, he was using a simplified, educational operating system called MINIX. Frustrated by its limitations, he decided to create his own operating system kernel from scratch as a personal hobby project.

He famously announced his project on a Usenet newsgroup, stating, "I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be big and professional like gnu) for 386(486) AT clones." This humble project, eventually named Linux, combined with tools from the GNU Project, grew into the powerful, open-source operating system that now powers the vast majority of servers, Android phones, and supercomputers worldwide. üåç

## The Tool: Git (2005)

Fourteen years later, Linus needed a new tool to manage the very project he had created. The development of the Linux kernel involved thousands of developers from around the world. They were using a proprietary version control system (VCS) called BitKeeper.

In 2005, the company behind BitKeeper revoked the free license for the Linux development community. Faced with a crisis, Linus did what he does best: he built his own solution. In just over a week, he created the initial version of Git, a new kind of VCS. His design goals were speed, a distributed model, and the ability to handle a massive project like the Linux kernel with integrity. Today, Git is the undisputed standard for version control in software development. üíª

The Connection? Pragmatism. Both Linux and Git were born out of Linus Torvalds' personal need for a better tool. He didn't set out to change the world; he just wanted to solve a problem he was facing. His genius was creating solutions that were so effective, they solved the same problem for millions of others. He wrote both in the C programming language. üí°
